📌 Statistical analysis.

My relationship with statistics dates back more than 10 years, starting from my first class at the University of Buenos Aires. Beyond my conceptual interest and analysis in research environments, I applied my knowledge during my time at CEPAL, where I contributed to the development of a Consumer Price Index (CPI) and other economic indicators in collaboration with Argentine government organizations.

More recently, as part of my doctoral thesis, I worked on models for categorical, binomial, and multinomial variables to analyze data from online surveys, which I not only analyzed but also designed and distributed using Qualtrics. The final analysis in the thesis utilized a complex version of the proportional odds model (McCullagh 1980), which allows representing ordinal categorical variables (such as Likert scale) as dependent variables. Just for illustrative purposes, the following graph shows how different satisfaction levels associated with various modes of collaboration vary according to the age of collaborators.

📌 Social network analysis in R and Gephi.

While my primary focus has been on statistical, descriptive, and inferential analysis, I’ve also delved into graph theory and social network analysis. Although I didn’t extensively research it due to the requirements of my thesis, I’ve utilized packages like Bibliometrix and others to measure collaborations between academic and business organizations through joint publications.

The endeavor wasn’t highly successful because very few businesses publish, but it did serve as a means to examine collaborations between academic organizations and practice network analysis. This allowed me to explore collaborations in the academic sphere and practice network analysis. For instance, I’ve been able to depict the evolution of collaborations in academic publications among various organizations in the city of Bariloche, Argentina. Below, you can find two graphical representations of collaborations in Bariloche, one in network form (left) and the other geographically (right). Qualitative data analysis

Besides employing the quantitative approach, I’ve also engaged extensively with qualitative methods. Although my exposure to this area is not as extensive, I’ve found their significant capability in offering intricate insights and contextual information.

📌 Interviews and case studies.

I had conducted interviews for my master’s thesis, and more recently, for my doctoral thesis, I analyzed 26 interviews using Atlas.ti. The preparation of interviews, contacting interviewees, conducting the interviews, and transcription are essential parts of this process (rubin2011?; kvale1994?). For the analysis, I employed thematic analysis (Braun and Clarke 2006), based on a list of a priori codes, which I later updated with emerging themes. Analyzing interviews can be a demanding task, but it provides valuable insights from multiple individuals with direct experience in the subject of study. Below is a screenshot of the thematic analysis process in Atlas.ti. In the future, I am excited about the prospect of conducting focus groups.

Mixed-methods analysis

I had my first experience with mixed methods during my doctoral thesis, following the guidance of (creswell2017?) and (tashakkori1998?). Despite their complexity in design and implementation, mixed methods offer an alternative that allows us to combine the strengths of both quantitative and qualitative approaches, and even incorporate other methodologies and theoretical frameworks.

📌 Quantitative and qualitative data integration.

In my case, I adapted and implemented an “explanatory sequential case study design”. Starting with the selection of a specific case study, this design initiates with a quantitative phase to identify aggregated behavioral patterns. The analysis of these results then guides the subsequent qualitative phase, aimed at achieving a deeper and more nuanced understanding of the aggregated patterns. Finally, the results from both phases are integrated, providing a more comprehensive outcome.
